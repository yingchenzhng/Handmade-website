Farewells & Good Luck

[Stanzas]

        
             I'll not weep that thou art going to leave me,
                There's nothing lovely here;
                     And doubly will the dark world grieve me,
                         While thy heart suffers there.
               

             
               I'll not weep, because the summer's glory
               Must always end in gloom;
               And, follow out the happiest story—
               It closes with a tomb!
               

                    And I am weary of the anguish
                    Increasing winters bear;
                    Weary to watch the spirit languish
                    Through years of dead despair.
                    

                    So, if a tear, when thou art dying,
                     Should haply fall from me,
                    It is but that my soul is sighing,
                To go and rest with thee.

About the Poet

1894–1962

Emily Brontë's only published poems during her lifetime were included in a slim volume titled Poems

by Currer, Ellis, and Acton Bell, which sold only two copies and received three reviews.

The reviews were positive, particularly for the contributions of Ellis Bell, which was

the pen name for Emily Brontë. Although it seemed that her potential was not fully realized before

her death in 1848, a collection of nearly 200 of Brontë's poems was later published in

The Complete Poems of Emily Jane Brontë. The publication of a complete and accurately

edited collection of Brontë's poems was delayed due to several factors, including

Charlotte Brontë's revisions and the wide dispersal of Brontë's manuscripts. The poems range

from 1836 to 1846 and reveal that Brontë had indeed reached great heights in her poetry
